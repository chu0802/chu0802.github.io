Statement of Purpose

Throughout my academic journey, I have been driven by a vision: enhance the adaptability of AI models and their interactive abilities within real-world scenarios across diverse domains and modalities. This passion guides my research in computer vision and AI, leading to first-author publications at CVPR 2023 [], and ECCV 2024 [], a co-authored paper at NeurIPS 2024 [], and an ongoing first-author submission for CVPR 2025 []. In this statement, I will detail my past research experiences and outline my potential future directions for my PhD career.

Domain Adaptation. During my master's studies under the supervision of Professor Lin, I became fascinated with AI adaptability, particularly in the context of Semi-Supervised Domain Adaptation (SSDA). Recognizing the limitations of models in transferring knowledge from labeled source domains to unlabeled target domains, I authored "Semi-Supervised Domain Adaptation with Source Label Adaptation," presented at CVPR 2023. In this project, we hypothesized DA as a Noisy Label Learning problem with a massive amount of data with noisy labels (source labels) and many unlabeled data. Then, we introduced a framework, Source Label Adaptation (SLA), to dynamically adapt noisy source labels to be more suitable for the target domain. This source-to-target paradigm is entirely different from traditional target-to-source DA algorithms, allowing us to cooperate with previous state-of-the-art methods, leading to significant improvements.

Continual Learning for Vision-Language Models. After obtaining my master's degree, I joined the Vision Learning Lab at National Taiwan University (NTU), led by Prof. Yu-Chiang Frank Wang, as a research assistant. Under Prof. Wang's guidance, I worked on continual learning and vision-language models (VLMs), culminating in my ECCV 2024 paper, "Select and Distill: Selective Dual-Teacher Knowledge Transfer for Continual Learning on Vision-Language Models." Addressing the challenge of fine-tuning pre-trained VLMs without compromising their zero-shot capabilities, we developed a Selective Dual-Teacher Knowledge Transfer framework. By leveraging both the fine-tuned and original pre-trained VLMs as dual teachers and implementing a selective knowledge distillation mechanism, our approach effectively preserved prior knowledge and zero-shot performance. This work reinforced my commitment to developing a continual multi-modal AI learner with adaptability to real-world applications.

Temporal Reasoning for Multi-Modal Models. During my role at the Vision Learning Lab, I also collaborated on the project "ReXTime: A Benchmark Suite for Reasoning-Across-Time in Videos," presented at NeurIPS 2024. We tackled the significant gap in AI models' temporal reasoning abilities across video segments, primarily due to the lack of suitable datasets for training multimodal large language models. To address this challenge, we developed an automated pipeline to generate temporal reasoning question-answer pairs, significantly reducing the need for manual annotations. By curating a benchmark with around 10,000 machine-generated training datasets and approximately 3,000 carefully vetted validation and test samples, we provided a rigorous testbed that revealed a substantial limitations in the performance of current state-of-the-art video understanding models on temporal reasoning tasks. This project deepened my understanding of the limitations of current multi-modal models and their potential to temporal reasoning applications.

Open-domain 3D articulation Synthesis. Most recently, I am a visiting student researcher at the Vision Learning Lab at the University of California, Merced, under the mentorship of Professor Ming-Hsuan Yang. Here, I worked on "Towards Affordance-Aware Articulation Synthesis for Rigged Objects," which is under review for CVPR 2025. Recognizing the labor-intensive process artists face in articulating open-domain 3D objects, we tackled the novel challenge of automating this task without the availability of training data. We developed A3Syn, a method that synthesizes articulation parameters for open-domain rigged objects based on the environment and textual prompts. By utilizing a 2D inpainting diffusion model and a well-developed semantic correspondence model, we achieved efficient posture alignment for open-domain objects. This project shows my commitment to enhancing AI's interactive capabilities within complex environments and bridges the gap between AI and practical creative workflows.
